# 7 Period Problems You Should See a Doctor About \(and When to Worry\)

Period problems such as an irregular cycle or pain are common, and often, they aren't anything to be concerned about. But when is it normal, and when should you seek medical advice? Women often feel that they should just accept period problems as 'part of being female', but it could be a sign of medical issues that can be treated. Here are the period problems you should see a doctor about …

## 1. Heavy Periods

If you suffer from heavy periods, or they have become unusually heavy, see your doctor. You could have a condition like [endometriosis](https://health.allwomenstalk.com/signs-of-endometriosis), where the lining of the uterus grows outside it, fibroids, or hormonal problems. It's important to get checked out as heavy bleeding could cause you to become anaemic.

## 2. Irregular Cycle

We're not all as regular as clockwork; menstrual cycles can vary and it's not always reason for concern. However, you should consult your doctor if your periods last longer than a week, are very heavy, or you're having a period more than once every 28 days. An irregular cycle could be caused by a health problem, or by your [method of contraception](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/contraception/).

| Symptom | Normal Range | When to see a doctor |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Cycle Length | 21–35 days | Less than 21 or more than 35 days |
| Period Duration | 2–7 days | Lasting longer than 7 days |
| Flow Volume | 30–60 ml | Soaking through a pad/tampon every hour |

## 3. You Haven't Started Your Periods

Women are starting menstruation much earlier than they used to, although there's no set time when it should begin. Starting your periods isn't a competition, so don't worry if your friends have begun theirs but you haven't yet got yours. But if you get to the age of 15 without starting your period, get checked out by your doctor, especially if you haven't had any other signs of puberty.

## 4. Painful Periods

Don't think that you should "put up with" painful periods as part of being a woman. There are lots of ways of alleviating discomfort, and it's worth trying these out to see if they help. However, if the pain is sudden or extreme, you have an IUD, or are experiencing other symptoms such as fever, consult your doctor.

Here are a few ways to manage mild discomfort:

- Using a heating pad or hot water bottle on your lower abdomen.
- Taking over-the-counter [painkillers](https://health.allwomenstalk.com/natural-period-pain-relief) as directed.
- Gentle exercise or yoga to help relax the muscles.
- Taking a warm bath.

## 5. Bleeding between Periods

Bleeding between periods is often known as 'spotting', because it's usually very light. But it can also be heavier. There are lots of reasons for bleeding between periods; it could be caused by fibroids or polyps, or if you've just started the contraceptive pill. See your doctor if you're concerned.

## 6. Missed Periods

Pregnancy is the obvious cause for a missed period, but there are any number of possible causes. If you're physically very active or under a lot of stress, you may find that you miss a period. It can also happen if you are underweight or have an eating disorder, or come off birth control. See your doctor if you have any symptoms you're worried about. If your periods are consistently irregular, check out our section on [Irregular Cycles](#2).

## 7. You Have Fever or a Rash

Do you use tampons? If you experience a fever or a rash during your period, get medical attention immediately. This could be a sign of [Toxic Shock Syndrome](https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/toxic-shock-syndrome/symptoms-causes/syc-20355384) (TSS), which is often associated with the use of tampons. It's a rare disease but you should always get checked out.

Some period problems are a question of learning to manage them, such as 'normal' pain. But it's always best to see your doctor if you're uncertain, and have any more serious problems ruled out. And if you do have a medical issue, it can get treated.
